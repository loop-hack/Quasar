Universal Human Values (UHV) aims to help individuals understand themselves, their relationship with others, nature, and the broader universe. It goes beyond academic knowledge to shape the character, attitude, and behaviour of a person. In today's rapidly changing world, where technology and materialism dominate, value education has become absolutely essential.

Value education is needed because: 1. Technical education alone cannot make a person good or happy. 2. Society needs ethical leaders, professionals, and citizens. 3. Environmental crises demand a change in human attitude, not just technology. 4. Healthy families, communities, and nations are built on shared values. 5. True happiness comes from right understanding and right relationships.

Justice in human relationships means: being seen, heard, valued, and treated rightly. Justice is not an external law imposed by courts - it is an internal recognition of the equal worth of every human soul. UHV identifies four orders of natural existence, each with its own distinct innateness.

Innateness of the Animal Order (Jantu Avastha) Essential Innateness: Consciousness and Herd/Instinctive Behaviour Animals have innate drives for survival, bonding, territory, and care of offspring. When forests are destroyed, this innateness is violated. Deforestation destroys capacity of ecosystems to sustain life.

Competence is the foundation - without it, no meaningful professional work is possible. Skill is the practical, hands-on ability to apply knowledge in real situations. Attitude refers to the professional's commitment to use their knowledge and skill in a responsible, honest, and beneficial way.

Professionals function on trust. Professional ethics are what make this trust rational and justified. When professionals act ethically, they build a reputation of integrity. This attracts talented people into the field, earns public respect.

Collegiality requires genuinely respecting the expertise, experience, and contributions of every colleague - regardless of their rank, gender, age, or background. A collegial professional does not hoard knowledge to maintain a competitive advantage. Instead, they freely share expertise, mentor junior colleagues, and contribute to the collective growth of the team.

Social responsibility means recognising that engineering decisions do not exist in a vacuum - they affect real people, real communities, and the natural environment. The foremost social responsibility of an engineer is to protect the safety of the public. Engineers have a responsibility to minimise the environmental impact of their work.

Engineers hold enormous power to shape human life and the natural world. With this power comes the responsibility to: protect public safety above all other considerations. Care for the environment - now and for future generations. Anticipate and mitigate tech risks (AI, data, etc.) Share expertise in public and policy forums.
